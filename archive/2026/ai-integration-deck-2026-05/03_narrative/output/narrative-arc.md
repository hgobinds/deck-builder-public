# Narrative Arc: AI Integration Plan

## Core Thesis

AI doesn't replace consultants. It removes the layer of work that gets in the way of consulting — so you can spend more time on judgment, diagnosis, and client trust.

## Audience Starting Point

Consultants and project leads. Mixed feelings about AI: some curious, some sceptical, most wondering what it means for their role. They are not opposed to AI — they are waiting to see whether it is real and relevant to their actual work.

## Tension

The fear is replacement. The reality is that the highest-value parts of consulting — reading a client, diagnosing the real problem, building trust, making judgment calls — cannot be automated. What can be automated is everything around that: setting up workspaces, logging meeting notes, formatting deliverables, routing outputs to the right place, drafting status updates.

The AI integration plan is built on this distinction.

## Argument Sequence

**1. The context (1 slide)**
AI is changing how professional services work. This is not a future prediction — it is happening now, including inside the organization.

**2. The reframe (1 slide)**
The question is not "will AI replace consultants?" The question is "which parts of consulting work should AI handle, and which parts should stay with the consultant?" The answer is clear: AI handles the operating layer. The consultant handles the judgment layer.

**3. What the operating layer looks like (1–2 slides)**
Walk through the consultant's actual work loop:
- Intake + Discovery
- Scope + Proposal
- Delivery
- Review + Client Comms
- Handoff

At each step, there is repetitive, forgettable, error-prone admin that AI can handle — and a core of diagnosis and judgment that stays human.

**4. What the tools look like (1–2 slides)**
Specific examples grounded in the toolkit proposal. Not all 15 tools — pick the most immediately relatable:
- Intake Brief Builder: turns a messy client email into a structured brief
- Meeting Notes Processor: turns a transcript into decisions, action items, and risks
- Review Gate: checks a deliverable before it leaves your desk
- Handoff Package Builder: creates documentation so the client can actually use what you built

Design principle: each tool does one job. The consultant doesn't have to remember where things go.

**5. Where we are now (1 slide)**
A sub-trial of a single-use toolkit for sample implementation projects is already underway. Two consultants have been using the system in a live engagement. We'll hear from them at the end of this session.

**6. The plan (1 slide)**
Three phases:
- Phase 1 (now): Core delivery tools — intake, scope, notes, review, comms, handoff
- Phase 2 (next): Control and memory tools — change detection, workspace audit, decision log
- Phase 3 (later): Growth tools — proposals, case studies, practice analytics

**7. What this means for you (1 slide)**
You do not need to change how you think about consulting. You need to start using the tools where they fit. The consultants who adopt this early will spend more time on the work that only they can do.

**8. Handoff to trial participants (1 slide)**
Introduce the two consultants running the sample implementation trial. Hand over to them.

## Objections to Handle

- "This is just another tool I have to learn" → Each tool does one job at one point in the workflow. You don't need to learn a new system — you need one tool for one task.
- "What if the AI gets it wrong?" → Every tool has a review step. The consultant stays in control.
- "Is my job at risk?" → Address directly: the consultants who will be most at risk are those who keep doing the parts AI can do better — and have no time left for the parts only humans can do.

## Desired Outcome

Consultants leave with a clear picture of what the AI integration plan looks like, why it is built the way it is, and what they should do next. The two trial participants make it real.
